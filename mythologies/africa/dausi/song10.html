The Curse of the Kingdom

Story

After the fall of Lagadu, the curse laid upon the land did not vanish. The Dausi epic tells us that the kingdom's collapse was not the end, but the beginning of a long spiritual sickness that echoed through the land, its people, and the bloodlines of kings.

This curse was not a thunderbolt — it was slow erosion, a quiet unraveling of balance. Rivers flowed, but crops withered. Children were born, but dreams faded. The dead did not sleep, and the living no longer sang with joy. Sacred groves became cold, drums cracked without being struck, and even the griots sang with hesitation.

It was believed that the land itself remembered — that the soil rejected the unworthy, and the gods withdrew their presence. The curse was the consequence of sacrificing what was sacred: sons for power, truth for pride, and legacy for greed.

To this day, the Dagomba people say that where Lagadu once stood, there is a weight in the wind — and that some songs bring more silence than sound.
